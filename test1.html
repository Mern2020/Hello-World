Jane Matilda Bolin, LL.B. (April 11, 1908 â€“ January 8, 2007) was the first black woman to graduate from Yale Law School, the first to join the New York City Bar Association and the first to join the New York City Law Department. She became the first black woman to serve as a judge in the United States when she was sworn into the bench of the New York City Domestic Relations Court in 1939.

Quick facts

Bolin was an activist for children's rights and education. She was a legal advisor to the National Council of Negro Women. She served on the boards of the NAACP, the National Urban League and the Child Welfare League. She received honorary degrees from Tuskeegee Institute, Williams College, Hampton University, Western College for Women and Morgan State University.


Here's a timeline of Jane Bolin's life



  • Jane Bolin was born on April 11th, 1908 in Poughkeepsie, NY

  • Bolins mother whom was an immigrant from the British Isles died when Bolin was 8. Bolin's father practiced law in Dutchess County for fifty years and was the first black president of the Dutchess County Bar Association.

  • As the child of an interracial couple, Jane Bolin was frequently subject to discrimination. She was occasionally denied services at busniess.

  • At 16 she enrolled in Wellesley College in Massachusetts and became one of two black freshmen on campus

  • She graduated in 1928 in the top 20 of her class. Although she was discouraged from doing so by her college adminitration because of her race and gender, she enrolled at Yale Law School where she was the only black student, and one of only three women

  • She became the first black woman to receive a law degree from Yale in 1931 and passed the New York state bar examination in 1932.

  • On July 22, 1939, at the New York World's Fair, Mayor of New York City Fiorello La Guardia appointed 31-year-old Bolin as a judge of the Domestic Relations Court. For twenty years, she was the only black female judge in the country.

  • She remained a judge of the court for 40 years, with her appointment being renewed three times, until she was required to retire aged 70.

  • During her time as a judge, she worked to encourage racially integrated child services, ensuring that probation officers were assigned without regard to race or religion, and publicly funded childcare agencies accepted children without regard to ethnic background

  • Jane Bolin died January 8, 2007 at the age of 98 in Queens, New York
